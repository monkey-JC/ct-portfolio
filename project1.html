Project 1 - Portfolio Website Design

Reflection

This term we were tasked with developing and designing our very own portfolio websites. It was the first proper webpage I had ever built so I came into it with no prior experience but a lot of determination. We started the process as a class, learning how to wireframe and making some sketches of our own. I regularly shared my ideas with classmates and friends, trying to learn from them and gain feedback. At the start, I wanted my website to have a minimalistic banner photo that popped up as soon as you clicked on the site, when you scrolled down it would show the different projects lined up with a short title in the centre. I later found out that this design was very boring after giving feedback to my classmates, as I was very eager to start coding, I had the misconception that my surface-level planning was enough. After realising my mistake, I went back to the drawing board and sketched my final design. The final product consists of the original banner image with a twist. It has a button in the bottom centre of the photo that takes you to the main page. Here, the project cards are stacked evenly with a short description coming up on hover. The design of this page is also much more minimalistic than my original plan, having a clear header and footer bar on top.

Challenges and Solutions

However, during the designing and developing process (especially the developing) I encountered many problems with my webpage. First was the issue of the information appearing on hover, at the time I could not figure out why my .info div was not appearing on the project cards. After consulting my father, I realised that the problem lay with my incorrect use of . By ending the division early, the css property was not able to affect all .info divisions. Second, was the colour scheme. I took the appearance of my webpage with great importance since it was the main component that would keep the viewer engaged. I searched up colour psychology wheels and a very useful website called ‘Coolors’. Here, I was able to find the most suitable shades that would work well with my overall design. The last major problem I ran into was the banner photo not covering the entire webpage. After applying background-size: cover and object-fit: cover, the photo was still leaving a gap, allowing for the background colours to be seen. This issue would have ruined the effect I was wanting with the banner photo. Since I was not able to solve it on my own, I noted the point down and asked my teacher in the next lesson, learning that the problem was a client-sided margin error. By using inspect element, my teacher showed me how google automatically applied a small margin to prevent overflow, he guided me into solving the issue via the margin: 0px command, effectively overriding the code placed by the browser. In the end, my design was quite ambitious with my limited knowledge of code, so with many google searches and W3Schools lessons I was able to come up with this final webpage you are looking at. This course helped me to greatly improve my troubleshooting, formatting and overall coding skills, learning how to design, develop and publish a very engaging, appealing and responsive website for users and the public alike.

Tools Used

During the developing process, I used Git and Github to constantly commit my changes onto a repository, this allowed me peace of mind since my hard-work would always be backed on the cloud. During the designing process, I used an app called ‘Goodnotes’ on my ipad to help with wireframing. To code the actual website, I used Visual Studio Code (VSC) and occasionally Sublime Text.

Final Note

Although I would have liked to improve more parts to create a polished design and cut down on bloating in my css, I am still very proud of my end product - and I hope you liked it too :)
